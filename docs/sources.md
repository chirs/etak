# Etak source notes

Reference material for making the visualization historically accurate, gathered from a
fact-checked research pass over the sources listed at the bottom. Each claim below is tagged
with a rough confidence based on how well the sources corroborated it:

- **[verified]** — stated in a primary/academic source and survived adversarial checking.
- **[caution]** — attested but thinly sourced (single site, or secondary-only).
- **[myth]** — commonly repeated but **not** supported by primary sources; do not use.

> Scope note: this is a *sourcing* document to drive the "use real maps" roadmap item. The full
> 32-point compass is now transcribed from Goodenough & Thomas (1987) — see §1.


## 1. The Carolinian sidereal star compass

**[verified]** The compass divides the horizon into **32 points ("star houses")**, named for the
points where stars rise and set. Crucially it is **not** made of 32 separate stars:

> **[myth]** "The compass is composed of thirty-two stars." — It is **32 *points*** generated by
> roughly **14–15 stars**, each observed at **both** its rising and its setting position
> (≈15 × 2 = 30 points), **plus two fixed anchors**: **Polaris** (north) and the **Southern
> Cross / Crux** (south). Refuted against the Penn Museum *Expedition* account.

**[verified]** Carolinian navigators lay the compass out with **east at the top**, anchored on
**Altair** — not north-up as in Western depictions. Altair is the "basic direction."

**[verified]** Structure around Altair: seven navigational stars rise **north** of Altair and
seven rise **south** of it; intermediate points are filled in by bisection until all 32 are marked.
Polaris fixes north, Crux fixes south.

**[verified]** Rising vs. setting points are distinguished by prefix in Carolinian: **`tan`** for
a rising point, **`tubul`** for a setting point. (Reciprocal star courses — a rising star and its
setting partner — are memorized as pairs; when the guiding star sets, the navigator follows its
partner across the sky.)

### The full 32-point compass

**[verified]** Transcribed from **Goodenough & Thomas (1987), Figure 2 and the star
identifications** (local copy: `refs/Native_Astronomy_in_the_Central_Carolines.pdf`, p. 5–6). Each
type-star names **two** points — its rising (east half) and its setting (west half) — plus the
four cardinals (Altair rising/setting are E/W; Polaris N and Crux upright S). Names are the
**Puluwat (#9) / Satawal (#10)** dialect forms from Goodenough's lists; `~` marks a form with
attested spelling variation or an identification the source itself flags as uncertain.

Azimuths are **computed**, not from the source: `az_rise = arccos(sin δ / cos φ)` at
φ = 7.4°N (Satawal), `az_set = 360 − az_rise`. Treat as approximate (±~1°). Note the native
compass calls **Altair's rising "due east" (`jeetiw`) by convention** even though its true azimuth
is ~81°, so conceptual bearings compress slightly toward the E–W axis versus these true values.

**Crux fine-positions (the southern gradation).** Between Scorpio's tail and due south the source
marks several Crux positions rather than one: Crux **rising** (~154°), Crux at **~45° elevation
east** (α Centauri rising), Crux **upright = due south** (`jeer`, 180°), and on the west side Crux
at **~45° west** (α Centauri near the meridian). These give the compass its finer southern
gradations — see Figure 2 for the exact placement. For the app, treating the 32 points as indices
0–31 with the azimuths above is sufficient; the Crux cluster can be simplified to rising / south /
setting if fine southern detail isn't needed.

### Altair etymology — correcting Gladwin's title

**[verified]** Gladwin's famous title *East Is a Big Bird* rests on a **mistranslation**. Per
Holton et al., "East is **Not** a 'Big Bird'" (*Oceanic Linguistics* 54(2), 2015): the star name
**Mailap** = *mai* + *-lap*, where **`-lap` means "primary/main/big"** and **`mai` does *not* mean
"bird"** (it traces to Proto-Chuukic *mai(a)* "taut" / *mai* "breadfruit"). So Mailap ≈ "the big
*mai*," not "big bird."

**[verified]** The genuine "big bird" is a **different** constellation — Woleaian **Mannap**
(literally "big bird"), a large figure centered on **Sirius**, spanning >58° from Procyon to
Canopus. Earlier literature conflated it with Altair.

→ *For the app:* keep the name **ETAK** and the compass, but if labeling Altair, use **"Mailap"**
and avoid repeating the "big bird" gloss as fact.


## 2. Etak — the reference-island method

**[verified]** **Etak** is a mnemonic/cognitive navigation method built on a **reference island**
(the *etak* island): an out-of-sight island chosen **to one side of the course line**, ideally
**~50 miles abeam** and roughly **midway** between departure and destination.

**[verified]** As the canoe sails, the navigator tracks the **star bearing** of that reference
island. The island appears to **move backward** under successive star points. **One etak = the
reference island moving back by one star point.** Each such step completes one segment of the
voyage (a 4-etak route is ¼ done after the first etak).

**[verified]** It is a **relative reference frame**: the canoe is held conceptually **at rest while
the etak island moves past it**. (This is exactly the app's CHART vs. NAVIGATOR toggle — the
NAVIGATOR frame is the authentic etak mental model.)

**[verified]** The navigational **stars used change during a leg**, because the reference island's
bearing sweeps across star points as the canoe advances. (This is the mechanic the app scores.)

**[verified]** Named etak stages tied to seabird range:
- **"drag/etak of birds"** — the outer limit where land-based (homing) birds feed at sea,
  given as **~30 km (≈18 mi)** from land; the **final two etaks** of a voyage.
- **"drag/etak of visibility" / "of sighting"** — the segment where the departure or target
  island passes into/out of naked-eye sight.

**[caution]** One source (canoeisthepeople.org, mirrored by Grokipedia) lists a fixed **six-etak
scheme** used for *all* Satawal voyages, with Carolinian stage names:
`etakin mwaan` (no more island birds) · `palsapou` (far from it) · `alugaan metaw` (middle of the
sea) · `sapalongon alugaan ponsapilong` (getting closer) · `etakin mwaan` (birds of the
destination) · `etakin kena` (looking for the island). **Single-source — treat as illustrative,
not authoritative.** Gladwin/Lewis describe a *variable* number of etaks per route, so a fixed six
is likely a local/simplified teaching model.

> **[myth]** "The literal meaning of *etak* is 'refuge'." — This comes from an **uncited Wikipedia
> lead**; every repetition traces back to it. Primary sources (Gladwin 1970; Lewis 1972; Gunn
> 1980) gloss *etak* as a **navigational segment / moving-reference method**, not "refuge." Don't
> use "refuge" anywhere in the UI.

**Documented courses & distances:**

- **[verified]** **Puluwat → Truk (Chuuk):** ~**150 mi (245 km)**, **nearly due east**, steered on
  **rising Altair**. Reference island **Pisaras** (~120 mi NE of Puluwat / ~120 mi NW of Truk),
  "dragged" from the rising of Vega through the rising of Cassiopeia. *(Best-attested worked
  example in the literature — good default demo passage.)*
- **[verified]** **Puluwat ↔ Satawal:** Puluwat lies roughly **halfway between Truk and Satawal**;
  Satawal is ~**130 mi WNW** of Puluwat (derive from coords above).
- **[caution]** **Satawal → Guam** (~500 nmi NW): steer on rising **Sirius** (~107° az), transition
  to **Canopus** (rising ~145°, setting ~217°); Altair setting (~278°) near Guam confirms latitude.
  *(Azimuths from a secondary/AI-summarized source — sanity-check before use.)*
- **[verified]** **Puluwat → Saipan (the 1969 revival):** the navigator **Hipour** (Weriyeng school,
  Puluwat) piloted David Lewis's ketch *Isbjorn* from Puluwat to Saipan **and back** using
  traditional methods only — the first traditional crossing of that seaway in ~60 years. Route:
  Puluwat → **Pikelot** (~100 mi stop), then ~**450 mi of open sea** to Saipan. Written up in
  Lewis, "Return Voyage Between Puluwat and Saipan Using Micronesian Navigational Techniques,"
  *JPS* 79(4), 1970, and in *We, the Navigators*. Note for the app: past the first ~100 mi there is
  **no real island positioned to segment the leg** — this is the seaway where the lore leans on
  **ghost islands** (Gunn 1980; Riesenberg 1975) and star courses with latitude checks, so *every*
  etak candidate scores mediocre. That is the instructive point, not a curation failure.

→ *For the app:* the **Puluwat → Truk on rising Altair, reference island Pisaras** run is the
cleanest authentic replacement for the generated puzzle — real endpoints, a real abeam reference,
and a documented star course.
